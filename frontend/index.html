        🛠️ The 7 Agent Tools

        Each agent autonomously chooses which tools to call and in what order, producing a unique decision path. Tools return real data — not mocked responses.

        view_product
        Fetches the full product detail page via HTTP, parses price, description, images, and specs. Agents use this to go deeper on items that catch their interest.
        compare_products
        Side-by-side comparison of up to 3 products from the page data. Triggers when an agent is deciding between options before committing.
        search_reviews
        Runs a live DuckDuckGo search for real customer reviews of the product. Results are injected back into the agent's context for the next decision.
        check_return_policy
        Fetches the site's return and shipping policy page. Risk-averse agents (typically luxury or budget) call this before purchasing.
        navigate_to_page
        Navigates to a different section of the store (sale, new arrivals, category pages). Enables multi-page shopping journeys rather than single-page snapshots.
        purchase
        Terminal action — agent declares a purchase with product name, price paid, and reasoning. Ends the simulation loop and logs a conversion event.
        leave
        Terminal action — agent exits the site with a detailed reason. This reason is the primary signal for drop-off theme classification and the UX report.

        🏁 Takeaways

        What this means for the future of e-commerce.

        AI-powered behavioral simulation isn't a novelty — it's the next step in a 30-year trend toward cheaper, faster, more granular customer understanding. Here's what matters most.

        1
        The insight gap is the conversion gap
        Most brands know their conversion rate is low but can't pinpoint why. Simulation closes that gap by generating a first-person narrative from every agent — making abstract drop-off data into specific, actionable feedback. The simulator doesn't just tell you that people leave; it tells you exactly what they were thinking when they did.
        2
        LLM tool-calling is the right abstraction for shopping
        Shopping is fundamentally a sequence of information-gathering decisions followed by a commitment. That maps perfectly to agentic AI: gather context via tools, reason about tradeoffs, decide. This isn't a prompt-engineering trick — it's multi-turn function calling with real HTTP requests, live web searches, and genuine decision loops. The fidelity is high because the mechanism matches the task.
        3
        Speed unlocks a new research cadence
        When a research cycle takes 6 weeks, teams run 2–3 studies per year. When it takes 10 minutes, teams can test before every deploy. This isn't an incremental improvement — it's a paradigm shift in how product decisions get validated. The teams that adopt this cadence first will compound their UX advantage faster than anyone using traditional methods.
        4
        Pricing is the most underused lever in e-commerce
        Most stores pick a price and rarely revisit it. But the A/B simulation reveals that different personas react to price changes differently — a 20% reduction might convert budget shoppers but have no effect on luxury buyers. Real-time price sensitivity testing across segmented personas is something no focus group or A/B split test can deliver without months of traffic and statistical significance requirements.
        5
        The architecture is extensible by design
        Custom personas, vision analysis, multi-page navigation, and SSE streaming were all added in layers without rewriting the core. The tool-calling pattern means new agent capabilities are just new functions — and the simulation pipeline means new output types (reports, visual analysis) are just new post-processing steps. This is a platform, not a one-trick tool.
